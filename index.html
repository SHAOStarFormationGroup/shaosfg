About

Welcome to the Star Formation and Interstellar Medium Astrophysics Group

We are a independent research team of researchers within the Astrophysics Division of the Shanghai Astronomical Observatory, led by Prof. Zhiqiang Shen. Our primary focus is on investigating various aspects of star formation and the interstellar medium (ISM) within galaxies.

Our Research Focus:

  • Stellar Birth:
    • Our group focus on star formation, from low-mass to high-mass stars within our galaxy and ISM.
  • Observational Expertise:
    • We employ advanced radio and sub-millimeter observation techniques and telescopes such as ALMA, SMA, JVLA, GBT, JCMT, and IRAM to support our exploration of the Galaxy.
  • Numerical Simulation:
    • Complementing our observations, we conduct simulations using cutting-edge radiation-magnetohydrodynamic (MHD) 3-D code. Our simulations operate within the block-structured Adaptive Mesh Refinement (AMR) framework of CHOMBO for parallel computation.

    Research

    ALMA three-millimeter observations of massive star-forming regions (ATOMS)

    The ATOMS, standing for ALMA Three-millimeter Observations of Massive Star-forming regions, survey has observed 146 active star-forming regions with ALMA band 3, aiming to systematically investigate the spatial distribution of various dense gas tracers in a large sample of Galactic massive clumps, to study the roles of stellar feedback in star formation, and to characterize filamentary structures inside massive clumps.

    An ALMA Campaign to Peer into the Central Molecular Zone

    The central 500 pc of our Galaxy, known as the 'Central Molecular Zone' or CMZ, contains a huge amount of dense molecular gas. Thanks to the extreme physical conditions around the Galactic Center, star formation in the CMZ shows very different characteristics compared to that in the solar neighborhood, for example, about 10 times lower star formation efficiency, and potentially top-heavy initial mass functions. To investigate the origin of the peculiar star formation activities in the CMZ, we have started an ALMA campaign to peer into molecular clouds, dense cores, and accretion disks in the CMZ. Our ongoing ALMA projects are investigating (from large to small scales): comprehensive excitation conditions in a cloud, a unique class of slim filaments, magnetic fields in dense cores, and fine structures inside a massive protostellar accretion disk, respectively.

    ALMA Survey of Orion Planck Galactic Cold Clumps (ALMASOP)

    The ALMASOP project, focusing on Planck Galactic Cold Clumps (PGCCs) within the Orion complex, utilized the Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) to conduct a comprehensive survey of 72 young dense cores. With three different configurations and high-resolution imaging at 1.3 mm, the project investigated the evolutionary stages and substructures of these cores, finding 70 substructures within 48 detected dense cores. Notably, the median dust mass was around 0.093 solar masses, and the deconvolved size was approximately 0.27 arcseconds. The study successfully detected dense substructures within the central 1000 astronomical units of four candidate prestellar cores. Additionally, the project observed changes in the sizes and masses of substructures as cores evolved from Class 0 to Class I in the protostellar development, contributing valuable insights into early star formation within PGCCs and the Orion complex.

    ORION2

    The formation of stars and stellar clusters remains a grand challenge problem in astrophysics that has important implications for the evolution of the interstellar medium as well as shaping the evolution of galaxies. The computational challenges are formidable and involve a coupling of highly non-linear physical processes such as hydrodynamics, self-gravity, magnetic fields, radiation transfer, supersonic turbulence, ionization, protostellar outflows, stellar winds, and chemistry that have both disparate timescales as well as operate over many decades of physical length scale. These processes can regulate the feedback from nascent protostars onto the surrounding turbulent gas clouds that are the embryos of new star formation, and as a result, the feedback itself can influence the gaseous reservoir feeding newly formed protostars which in turn influence the star formation process.
